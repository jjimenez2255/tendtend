# Tend Strategist Analysis v2
## March 17, 2026

## What changed from V1

- **Mission Control is no longer part of the public sale.** It stays internal for now. That means Tend should stop selling a dashboard story and start selling a managed service story.
- **Alfred is now clearly internal.** Alfred is the internal orchestrator / COO agent managing expert agents behind the scenes, not a public-facing brand.
- **SupportClaw stays behind the curtain.** Clients buy support and continuity, not another product name.
- **The default offer is now single-agent.** This is the right move. It lowers buying friction, shortens the sales cycle, and gives Tend a clean entry offer.
- **Multi-agent becomes the expansion path, not the front-door requirement.** Sell one valuable workflow first, then expand into orchestrated specialist agents after proof.
- **Target market is sharper.** Focus on SMBs, especially mid-market teams with real budget and operational pain. Avoid both tiny budget buyers and heavy procurement enterprise.
- **Control language stays, but the borrowed authority gets toned down.** Use OpenShell / NemoClaw vocabulary where factual and useful: scoped access, approvals, auditability, orchestration, controlled execution. Do not lean on NVIDIA halo marketing.
- **The $5K / $2K floor remains viable only if the scope is tight.** The floor cannot mean “custom AI magic.” It has to mean one narrowly-defined production workflow with managed operation.
- **A public methodology page is now a good idea.** But it should explain the process, not expose the factory.

## Decisions now locked

- **MC is internal-only for now.** No client dashboard promise in the core offer.
- **Alfred is internal-only.** Alfred is the operating orchestrator for Tend’s internal delivery stack.
- **SupportClaw is internal-only.** Publicly, this shows up as support, monitoring, and managed continuity.
- **Standard entry offer = one agent.** This is the default package.
- **Expansion offer = multi-agent system.** Recommended once the first workflow proves value.
- **Target buyer = SMB / mid-market teams.** Prioritize teams with urgency, clear workflow pain, and ability to pay.
- **Pricing floor stays at $5K setup / $2K monthly.** Scope must match that floor.
- **Public language should use controlled-agent vocabulary.** OpenShell / NemoClaw style concepts are useful; fake enterprise posturing is not.
- **Methodology page is allowed.** Keep it high-level, practical, and non-replicable.

## Recommended offer architecture

Tend should sell **managed AI workflow operators** with a clear ladder:

### 1. Entry Offer: Single-Agent Deployment
This is the default sale.

What the client is buying:
- one agent
- one high-value workflow
- defined permissions and approvals
- managed setup, operation, and support

What Tend is really delivering behind the scenes:
- internal orchestration via Alfred
- internal oversight via Mission Control
- internal support operations via SupportClaw-style processes

This is the right front door because it is easier to understand, easier to buy, and easier to prove ROI on.

### 2. Expansion Offer: Multi-Agent System
Once the first agent is working, Tend expands the account into:
- one orchestrator
- multiple scoped expert agents
- cross-workflow coordination
- broader business coverage

This is where Tend increases ACV. Do not force this on day one. Earn the right to expand.

### 3. Internal Delivery Stack
Keep the real machinery internal:
- **Alfred** = orchestration and operational management
- **Mission Control** = internal visibility, workflow tracking, audit surface, delivery ops
- **Configurator / factory layer** = internal build system
- **Support function** = monitoring, incident handling, tuning, continuity

Publicly, Tend should look simple. Internally, Tend should be sophisticated.

### 4. Commercial Framing
The public offer should be framed like this:
- **We identify one painful workflow**
- **We deploy a controlled AI operator into that workflow**
- **We run and support it with human oversight**
- **Then we expand only when the first use case proves itself**

That is the cleanest architecture for Tend right now.

## Floor build at $5K/$2K

The floor build should be strict, concrete, and profitable.

### Setup: $5K
Includes:
- discovery and workflow selection for **one** business function
- workflow map and operating design for **one** primary use case
- deployment of **one production agent** with a tightly-scoped role
- up to **2 integrations** with existing tools
- one operating channel for the team to work with the agent
- permissions, escalation logic, and approval gates for sensitive actions
- context / knowledge setup from existing SOPs, docs, FAQs, and examples
- internal testing, launch, and first-pass tuning
- one team onboarding session

The floor build should aim at use cases like:
- inbound lead qualification and routing
- meeting prep and follow-up
- support triage
- internal ops coordination
- reporting and status synthesis
- simple CRM/admin workflow handling

### Monthly Managed Service: $2K
Includes:
- monitoring and operational oversight
- prompt / context / routing tuning
- incident handling and fix-forward support
- light workflow improvements within the original scope
- monthly performance review with recommendations
- ongoing human-in-the-loop support for edge cases and exceptions

### Explicit exclusions from the floor
Not included at $5K / $2K:
- custom client dashboard
- multi-agent deployment
- broad cross-department automation
- net-new software product development
- large integration programs
- full compliance / procurement requirements
- “replace an entire team” promises

The rule is simple: **the floor sells one controlled workflow that actually ships.** If the client wants broader transformation, that becomes the expansion package.

## Messaging implications for landing page

The landing page should now do five things clearly:

### 1. Sell the right thing
Do not sell a dashboard.
Do not sell a lab.
Do not sell internal product names.

Sell this:
- we build, run, and support AI agents for real business workflows
- we start with one valuable workflow
- we expand once the first deployment proves value

### 2. Speak to the right buyer
The copy should target:
- busy operators
- heads of ops
- founders with overloaded teams
- mid-market teams dealing with repetitive workflow drag

This buyer cares about throughput, reliability, and control. Not AI theatre.

### 3. Use control language without cosplay
Use phrases like:
- scoped access
- approval gates
- human oversight
- controlled execution
- audit-friendly operations
- managed deployment

Avoid pretending Tend is already a full enterprise compliance vendor.

### 4. Make the offer legible
The landing page should show a simple flow:
- pick the workflow
- deploy the agent
- run it with oversight
- expand when ready

That is much easier to buy than a vague “AI workforce” pitch.

### 5. Add a methodology page
The methodology page should explain:
- how Tend selects workflows
- how Tend scopes permissions and approvals
- how Tend deploys safely
- how Tend monitors and improves performance

Keep it high-level. Enough to build trust. Not enough to hand over the factory.

## What NOT to do

- **Do not make MC the centerpiece of the public pitch.** It is internal for now. Selling it publicly creates expectation debt.
- **Do not brand-spam the client.** Alfred and SupportClaw should stay internal.
- **Do not lead with multi-agent complexity.** Lead with one agent, one workflow, one outcome.
- **Do not sell “AI employees.”** That framing creates bad expectations and invites skepticism. Sell controlled workflow operators.
- **Do not use NVIDIA / enterprise halo language as a crutch.** Use factual OpenShell / NemoClaw control concepts, not borrowed prestige.
- **Do not under-scope the $5K setup.** If the floor becomes custom consulting soup, margin dies.
- **Do not target tiny businesses with no budget.** The floor is still premium. Aim at teams that feel real operational pain.
- **Do not publish the factory.** Methodology page yes. Internal architecture no.

## Top 5 next actions

1. **Rewrite the landing page around the single-agent managed offer.** Remove any implication that the core sale is a dashboard or public control plane.

2. **Create the floor offer one-pager and scope sheet.** Define exactly what $5K setup and $2K monthly include, plus exclusions. This should become sales, delivery, and pricing discipline.

3. **Publish a high-level methodology page.** Show workflow selection, controls, deployment, and optimization. No internal stack names, no replicable factory details.

4. **Build the expansion ladder into sales materials.** Single-agent first, then multi-agent system upgrade once value is proven. Make the upsell path explicit.

5. **Operationalize the internal stack around this model.** Alfred runs orchestration internally, MC tracks delivery internally, and Tend presents one clean managed-service face to the client.
